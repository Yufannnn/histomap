# The Silk Road Wasn't a Road

Let's clear this up: the "Silk Road" was neither a single road nor primarily about silk. The name was invented in 1877 by German geographer Ferdinand von Richthofen. The people who actually used these routes for 1,500 years never called them that.

## What It Actually Was

The Silk Road was a shifting network of trails, mountain passes, oasis towns, and sea lanes connecting China to the Mediterranean. No one merchant traveled the whole thing — goods passed through dozens of hands, each middleman adding their markup.

A bolt of Chinese silk might take **two years** to reach Rome, changing hands 10-20 times along the way. By the time it arrived, the price had multiplied a hundredfold.

## It Wasn't Just Silk

The goods flowing along these routes were incredibly diverse:

- **Eastward:** Roman glass, gold, wine, wool
- **Westward:** Chinese silk, porcelain, paper, lacquerware
- **Both directions:** spices from India, horses from Central Asia, gems, religions, diseases, and ideas

Buddhism traveled from India to China along these routes. Islam spread east through them. The Black Death rode them westward in the 1340s.

## The Real Currency Was Information

The most valuable thing the Silk Road carried wasn't any physical good — it was knowledge. Papermaking techniques, mathematical concepts, astronomical observations, and medical knowledge all flowed along these routes, centuries before the internet connected the world.

In a real sense, the Silk Road was humanity's first internet — slow, unreliable, full of middlemen, but revolutionary in its impact.
