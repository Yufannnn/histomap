# Life is Just a Joke? — The Dark Humor of Bohemia

*An essay on Czech culture, gallows humor, and the philosophical roots of Bohemian absurdism — inspired by Crusader Kings III.*

---

## A Curiosity Sparked by Gameplay

This essay marks the beginning of what I hope will become a series, inspired not from any academic rigor but from Paradox Interactive's *Crusader Kings III* (CK3), a game that might just be my favorite, whose intricate historical landscapes and cultural textures have repeatedly tugged at my curiosity, compelling me to dig into their real-world roots. What began as idle musings when conquering duchies and managing succession crises has evolved into these written reflections: mostly for my own clarity, partly for fellow enthusiasts who share the same itch to uncover the stories behind the game, and hopefully for casual readers who enjoy drifting through random tales. I guess these pieces are not meant to be rigorous analyses or academic treatises but rather a casual collection of observations, thoughts drawn from my own humble knowledge, and on-the-fly research such that they might wander between topics without clinging to a single thesis.

![CK3 Czech Culture — "Life is Just a Joke" tradition](/writings/images/bohemia-ck3-culture.png)

For players of CK3, Paradox Interactive recommends beginning the 1066 AD campaign with the *"Rags to Riches"* scenario as **Vladislav II** of the **Přemyslid dynasty** — Duke of Bohemia and a vassal of the Holy Roman Empire for Vladislav's stewardship skills, the relative security of imperial vassalage, and his position as one of the Empire's seven electors offer both stability and ambitious pathways to power, particularly through the *House Seniority Succession Law*, a system I wish to dissect in the future. Yet after countless playthroughs as Bohemia's ruler, what has struck me most, amidst all its unique traits, is the cultural peculiarity laid bare when one switch the map to culture mode: Paradox designed the Bohemian (Czech) cultural ethos with a bewildering tradition, ***"Life is Just a Joke"***.

> *"This culture (Czech Culture) would chuckle on the chopping block with an axe above its head."*  
> — In-game tradition description, *Crusader Kings III*  

Interestingly, this tradition is absent from almost all other vanilla cultures in CK3 unless specifically derived from Czech roots or gained through manual revolutionizing culture, which suggests to me that, if not by mistake, Paradox intentionally created this tradition specifical for Czech as a unique reflection of Bohemia's historical-cultural psyche. This compelled me to interrogate both the historical roots of such a worldview and its existential implications — how might a medieval Bohemian's *gallows humor* relate to modern Czech society's philosophical DNA, where mortality and existence have been negotiated through cycles of defiance and despair? Through this exploration, I'll trace how **geography** (relative landlocked yet culturally porous), **history** (Hussite wars to Habsburg subjugation), and **artistic expression** (Kafkaesque absurdism to Švejkian satire) conspired to forge a culture where life's fragility is met not with terror, but wry laughter.

For consistency, I will use the term *"Bohemia"* to refer specifically to the historical kingdom and geographical region (prior to 1918), while *"Czech"* will encompass both the cultural tradition (including its medieval Bohemian roots) and the modern nation-state (post-1918). A caveat, however: the game focuses on the years starting from 867 AD, 1066 AD, and 1178 AD till 1453 AD (this year should have rung a bell), yet my research reveals scant direct evidence of Czech humor toward death in these early eras — most documented examples emerge centuries later. This suggests Paradox may have engaged in a form of *reverse cultural engineering*, extrapolating from modern Czech absurdist traditions (Kafka, Hašek) to imagine how medieval Bohemians might have framed mortality. While this essay occasionally bridges into later periods, such as exploring Hussite defiance or Habsburg-era satire, these detours aim not to conflate timelines, but to illuminate how a culture's relationship with death evolves. Although I have digressed somewhat from examining 11th-century Czech culture specifically to touching on later periods, these temporal leaps ultimately serve to reinforce, not dilute, the essay's core thesis.

## A Geography Shaped by Borders

As a proponent of *geographical determinism*, I believe geography writes the first draft of culture — and Bohemia, that mountain-ringed basin at Europe's crossroads, in my opinion, is yet another evidence proving the rule. To the north, the **Sudeten Mountains** rise as stoic guardians against frigid winds and Saxon incursions, their glacial valleys and plateaus — crowned by Mount Sněžka's snow-dusted peak — carving a rugged border. In the southwest, the **Šumava Forest** looms as a primeval wilderness, its labyrinth of ancient pines and crystalline rivers muffling Bavarian while nurturing Bohemia's mythic imagination. Southeastward, the **Moravian Hills** settle into fertile undulations, their valleys hinting at distant Hungarian horizons yet resolutely cradling Bohemia within its own pastoral basin. Through this basin pulse the **Vltava** and **Ohře**, twin arteries of commerce and conquest, their silt-rich banks birthing Prague's spires and Kutná Hora's silver mines.

Yet, Bohemia's mountain walls and river moats, while formidable, could not fully insulate it from the gravitational pull of neighboring empires. The land's paradox — *a fortress and a crossroads* — meant it became both battleground and broker, absorbing Germanic, Slavic, and Magyar influences while resisting total assimilation; this dance of vulnerability and resilience, evident from Habsburg crowns to Nazi boots to Soviet tanks, is where I trace the roots of its *"Life is Just a Joke"* ethos. This tension between foreign-imposed elites and a tenaciously rooted populace created a recurring dichotomy, one where power resided with outsiders while identity clung fiercely to the land itself. In the next section, *A Brief History of Bohemia*, we'll trace how this divide shaped rebellions, reforms, and ultimately, the Czech ethos of survival through defiant humor.

On a more personal note, I've always found Bohemia's borders a quiet masterpiece of natural cartography — its contours shaped not by the caprice of rulers, but by the invisible hand of geology. After countless hours spent studying various maps, I've come to appreciate the serene elegance of regions defined by smooth, unbroken natural borders, those carved by mountains, rivers, or coastlines, without the harsh interruptions of enclaves or jagged lines. Bohemia's silhouette, all unbroken arcs and seamless contours, epitomizes this ideal of *cartographic harmony*, resting like a polished gem in the Holy Roman Empire, a jewel cherished as much as it was constrained.

It may seem preposterous to some, but the aesthetic allure of Bohemia's map was another captivating force that drew me into the study of Bohemia. This allure finds a mirror in **Great Hungary**, whose map hums with a similar symphony of natural borders: the Carpathians curve like a stony crescent guarding its northern and eastern flanks, while the Transdanubian Mountains, Mátra's volcanic bones, Bakony's limestone ridges brace against the restless stirrings from beyond the Danube (I may well write a separate piece on Greater Hungary and the Magyars, if only to do justice to the sheer geographic elegance of Greater Hungary). In stark contrast, there are regions where the borders defy this aesthetical logic where awkward angles and artificial lines look as if they were drawn on map rather than shaped by nature, with Afghanistan's borders epitomizing this dissonance, particularly the **Wakhan Corridor**: a jagged, unnatural sliver of land snaking between Tajikistan and Pakistan like a cartographic afterthought. Namibia's frontiers are no less jarring — razor-straight lines cleaving through the landscape, their geometry abruptly ruptured by the **Caprivi Strip**'s protrusion.

## A History Forged in Resistance

According to Slavic legend, the Czech people trace their origins to the mythic patriarch **Čech**, one of three brothers — *Lech, Čech, and Rus* — who parted ways to found the Slavic nations of Poland, Bohemia, and Kyivan Rus. Immortalized in the 13th-century *Wielkopolska Chronicle*, the tale recounts their fateful divergence: Lech struck northward, drawn by the call of the white eagle that would become Poland's emblem; Rus ventured east into the boundless steppes, sowing the seeds of East Slavic identity; Čech led his followers west to the fertile Bohemian basin, where he ascended **Říp Mountain** and proclaimed the land *"flowing with milk and honey"*.

By the late 9th century, Bohemia's patchwork of tribal clans had coalesced into a centralized feudal state under the **Přemyslid House**, which is mirrored in CK3's mechanical shift from tribal to feudal administration between 867 AD and 1066 AD. This process climaxed under **Duke Bořivoj I** of the Přemyslid dynasty, who, in the late 9th century, spearheaded the Christianization that would cement Bohemia's integration into medieval Europe's political and spiritual fabric. Soon after, in 1002, **Duke Vladivoj** of Bohemia secured a pivotal concession from King Henry II of Germany, a formal enfeoffment of the duchy as an imperial estate of the Holy Roman Empire. This act of political theater — Bohemia already *de facto* under German sway — granted the Přemyslids legitimacy as imperial vassals while preserving remarkable autonomy, a delicate balance that would define Bohemia's place in the *Reich* for seven centuries. The duchy's status was further promoted in 1198 when **Ottokar I**, leveraging papal-imperial rivalries, extracted from Emperor Philip of Swabia the *Golden Bull of Sicily*, elevating Bohemia to a hereditary kingdom. This charter not only affirmed royal title but granted Bohemian kings electoral privileges, making Prague's rulers navigated dual sovereignty, swearing fealty to emperors while ruling as sovereigns at home.

Bohemia's position as a cultural chessboard — poised between Germanic and Slavic spheres yet anchored to the Holy Roman Empire — sparked a calculated transformation: the gradual **Germanization**. The *Ostsiedlung* ("eastward settlement"), a medieval demographic gambit, saw waves of German-speaking farmers, miners, and merchants flood into Bohemia's hinterlands. Přemyslid rulers like **Ottokar II** (the *"Iron and Golden King"*) and **Wenceslaus II**, ever the pragmatists, incentivized this migration, granting settlers German-law privileges in border zones like the Sudetenland and nascent mining towns (Stříbro, Kutná Hora).

![Kutná Hora Mine — CK3 screenshot](/writings/images/bohemia-germanization.png)

These settlers secured privileges under *ius teutonicum* (German law), accelerating the spread of German language, legal norms, and urban culture. Over generations, towns like Stříbro and Cheb evolved into predominantly German-speaking enclaves, their guildhalls and market squares mirroring Nuremberg more than Prague. Yet this cultural transformation met its counterpoint under **King Wenceslaus II** (Václav II), whose reign witnessed Bohemia's meteoric rise as Europe's silver capital with the 1298 discovery of colossal argentiferous veins at Kutná Hora triggered a mining frenzy — medieval Europe's equivalent of a gold rush. In 1346, **Charles IV** — born Václav, the son of John of Luxembourg and a Přemyslid princess, a Francophile educated in Paris who Latinized his name to bridge his Bohemian roots with imperial aspirations — ascended the throne, heralding Bohemia's zenith of medieval influence. Charles IV's reign is seen as Bohemia's *golden era*: he founded **Charles University** (1348), the first in Central Europe; made Prague the empire's capital with landmarks like **St. Vitus Cathedral**; and codified imperial law in the *Golden Bull of 1356*, securing Bohemia's electoral primacy. His true genius lay in balancing identities — promoting Czech St. Wenceslaus, courting German burghers, minting Bohemian lions, yet styling himself *Rex Romanorum*.

But golden ages seldom last forever, and beneath the surface of Charles IV's renaissance simmered deepening tensions — ethnic, linguistic, and religious — as German patricians dominated urban councils while the Czech-speaking rural majority clung fiercely to their Slavic tongue and traditions, widening a cultural schism soon compounded by rising religious discontent. Over time, this twin tension — between foreign culture elites and native traditions, between Catholic orthodoxy and reformist fervor — laid the kindling for outright revolt. The spark came in **1419**, when Catholic officials were thrown from a window of Prague's New Town Hall in what became known as the **First Defenestration of Prague**, igniting the **Hussite Wars**. These wars, Europe's first continent-wide religious conflict, pitted the Hussites, followers of the martyred reformer **Jan Hus**, against Crusader armies sent by Pope Martin V. Hus's demands — *vernacular liturgy, clerical poverty, communion for laity* — resonated with Czechs long chafing under German-Catholic hegemony. The Hussites' guerilla tactics and innovative *war wagons* humiliated imperial forces, forcing the Council of Basel to grant concessions in 1436, yet victory proved bittersweet: though Bohemia secured religious autonomy, the wars left it isolated, its economy scarred, and its people divided — a preview of the trauma to come. Lasting from 1419 to 1434, the Hussite Wars reshaped Bohemian society, establishing it as a center for religious reform and resistance to foreign intervention.

With the fires of religious conflict barely extinguished, Bohemia entered a new epoch in **1526** — no longer defined by rebellion but by imperial control, as the Habsburgs' ascension to the throne ushered in an era of uneasy stability, where economic growth unfolded under the shadow of simmering tensions between Czech aspirations and Vienna's iron grip. German tightened its hold as the language of power, relegating Czech to villages and clandestine meetings, while imperial policies eroded Bohemia's historic autonomy. This fragile equilibrium shattered with Martin Luther's *Disputation on the Power of Indulgences*, or *95 Theses*. In **1618**, when Protestant nobles, emboldened by Luther's Reformation and resentful of Habsburg absolutism, staged the **Second Defenestration of Prague**: two Catholic governors and their secretary were hurled from Prague Castle's windows (miraculously surviving, according to Catholic lore), igniting the **Thirty Years' War**, another continent-wide maelstrom of religion and *realpolitik*. For Bohemia, the conflict reached its brutal climax at the **1620 Battle of White Mountain**, where Habsburg forces crushed the Protestant nobility with reckoning aftermath of executions, confiscations, and forced re-Catholicization. By war's end in 1648, Bohemia's population had halved, its economy lay in ruins, and its identity was subsumed into the Habsburg monolith. Still, the soul of a nation cannot be easily erased, for while Bohemia's institutions bowed, its culture endured underground — in folk ballads, secret Protestant gatherings, and the dark humor of those who had learned to laugh when survival itself became absurd.

In more recent history, during World War II, Nazi Germany dissolved Czechoslovakia, establishing the **Protectorate of Bohemia and Moravia** — deemed *"Aryanizable"* by Nazi racial policies — Czechs faced forced labor, executions, and cultural erasure. Resistance crystallized in acts like **Operation Anthropoid** in 1942, where exiled Czechoslovak agents **Jan Kubiš** and **Jozef Gabčík** assassinated **Reinhard Heydrich**, the *"Butcher of Prague"*, which incurred horrific reprisals: the villages of **Lidice** and **Ležáky** were obliterated, their populations massacred or deported. Yet defiance persisted, culminating in the **1945 Prague Uprising**, where Czech partisans seized the city days before the Red Army's *"liberation"* — a pyrrhic victory that exchanged Nazi tyranny for Soviet dominance. The Soviet-led Warsaw Pact invasion crushed the movement, reinstating hardline control under Gustáv Husák yet even this defeat sowed seeds of dissent, blossoming in 1989's **Velvet Revolution**.

> The historical narrative of Bohemia in this section, though briefly outlined yet carefully selected, reveals a recurring pattern: a tension between ruling elites — Germanic monarchs, Habsburg emperors, and modern totalitarian regimes — and a marginalized majority, reflecting a persistent dialectic between *imposed authority* and *grassroots defiance*. Germanic rulers enforced cultural hegemony over Slavic majorities; Catholic Habsburgs suppressed Protestant dissent; Nazi and Soviet occupiers sought to erase Czech identity. Each regime provoked resistance met with brutal suppression — Hussite rebels crushed, White Mountain's Protestant nobility executed, Prague Spring reformers exiled. Yet from this cycle of rebellion and defeat emerged a cultural alchemy, transmuting despair into dark satire.

## A Culture Etched in Satire

When history denied them power and tragedy became routine, the Czechs answered not with despair but with a smirk, shaping a culture where survival itself became an art of subversion and wit. Over time, this reflex to transmute suffering into satire was distilled into narrative itself, giving rise to a literary tradition where resistance wore the mask of foolishness, and critique came cloaked in comedy. Jaroslav Hašek's ***The Good Soldier Švejk*** epitomizes this tradition. Švejk, the bumbling yet cunning soldier, navigates the absurdities of war and authority of the Austro-Hungarian Empire by playing the fool, undermining power structures through passive resistance. Scholar Peter Steiner argues that Švejk's mockery of militarism and bureaucracy reflects a *national survival tactic*, one born from centuries of political powerlessness (I really wish I have time in future to read the book). Similarly, Bohumil Hrabal's ***Too Loud a Solitude*** portrays a trash compactor who finds poetry in destruction, embodying the Czech ability to derive meaning from futility.

This literary instinct toward creative subversion — finding meaning in absurdity, poetry in destruction — has deep roots in the Czech popular imagination, flourishing in folk performances, especially in theater, where laughter served as camouflage for critique. Czech folklore and theater employed satire as resistance. The trickster figure **Kašpárek**, a staple of Czech puppetry, outwits authority with wit rather than force — a metaphor for a people who could not overthrow their rulers but could ridicule them. During the Habsburg repression, puppet shows became a covert means of preserving Czech language and subversive humor. This tradition persisted into the 20th century, with the **Czech New Wave** cinema blending tragedy and farce to critique authoritarianism.

Moving beyond the stage and the page, this cultural tendency toward darkness and defiance took form in the city's legends and geography. Prague's winding cobblestone streets and Gothic spires have also long been shrouded in eerie legends, earning it a reputation as Europe's unofficial *"ghost capital."* The city's turbulent history — marked by wars, executions, and alchemical experiments — has birthed countless spectral tales, from the phantom of the **Charles Bridge**, who lures victims into a fatal dance, to the restless **Golem of Prague**, a clay monster said to stalk the Jewish Quarter. Even the **Old Town Square**, once a site of public beheadings, is rumored to echo with the whispers of the dead. Unlike other European cities that embrace their haunted past as mere folklore, Prague wears its darkness with pride, blending history and myth into a macabre cultural identity. Czechs, no strangers to suffering, have turned these ghost stories into both entertainment and a defiant embrace of the absurd — after all, if history has taught them anything, it's that the dead might be the most reliable witnesses to the city's enduring spirit.

Nowhere is this union of death, beauty, and dark wit more tangible than in **Kutná Hora**, a town where memory is carved, quite literally, into bone. Deep in the quiet town of Kutná Hora lies one of Europe's most hauntingly beautiful sites — the **Sedlec Ossuary**, a small chapel adorned with the bones of over 40,000 souls. Its story began in the 13th century when soil from Jerusalem sanctified its cemetery, making it a coveted burial ground. Centuries later, plagues and wars filled it beyond capacity, leaving a grim surplus of skeletons. In 1870, the Schwarzenberg family commissioned **František Rint**, a local woodcarver, to bring eerie order to the chaos. The result? A chilling work of art — bone chandeliers containing every human bone, pyramids of skulls, and even a Schwarzenberg family crest meticulously pieced together from femurs and pelvises. Rint, proud of his macabre craftsmanship, signed his name in bones on the chapel wall. This ossuary is more than a morbid curiosity; it embodies the Czech people's unique relationship with mortality, a blend of defiance, dark humor, and reverence.

## A Philosophy Rooted in Absurdity

The Czech engagement with existential absurdity constitutes not merely an artistic or cultural expression, but also a profound *ontological orientation* — a way of being-in-the-world that transforms historical trauma into philosophical *praxis*. This tradition, crystallized in the works of **Kafka**, **Havel**, and **Kundera**, articulates what might be termed a ***"Bohemian existentialism"***: a mode of thought distinct from its Western counterparts in that it is not rooted in the isolated subject, but in a historically situated, collective negotiation of meaning under systems of domination.

At the heart of this outlook lies **Kafka**'s radical epistemological skepticism. His protagonists — Josef K. in *The Trial* and K. in *The Castle* — inhabit worlds where the Cartesian subject-object divide collapses, leaving the individual not merely alienated from power but suspended in a state of *ontological ambiguity*. In these narratives, even the categories of guilt and innocence, success and failure, dissolve into meaninglessness. As Deleuze and Guattari argue in *Kafka: Toward a Minor Literature*, this is not merely a literary critique of bureaucracy but a profound challenge to Western metaphysics itself — a deconstruction of the very possibility of stable meaning in language and institutions. They propose that Kafka's work exemplifies a *"minor literature,"* wherein a major language is deterritorialized and subverted from within, allowing marginalized voices to express collective concerns. (Interestingly, this philosophical resonance extends beyond literature into the realm of computer science, my own field of study. **Apache Kafka**, the distributed event streaming platform, was named after Franz Kafka by its creator, Jay Kreps, who found the name fitting for a system built to process real-time data streams with complex, often inscrutable interconnections — echoing the labyrinthine, disorienting structures that define Kafka's fictional worlds.)

Where Kafka leaves us suspended in interpretive uncertainty, **Václav Havel** translates that insight into moral action. His philosophy of *"living in truth"* renders Kafkaesque alienation politically actionable. Drawing on Heidegger's concept of *Sein-zum-Tode* (being-toward-death) and Levinas's ethical encounter with the *Other*, Havel exposes how totalitarian regimes depend on what he termed *"the auto-totality of the lie"* — a self-contained system of signs untethered from any grounding in reality. The seemingly trivial act of a greengrocer removing a propagandistic sign becomes, for Havel, a rupture in this ontological façade. In today's terms, this could be seen as a kind of *prefigurative politics* — a deliberate reshaping of reality through small, phenomenologically grounded refusals.

If Kafka maps the architecture of existential absurdity, and Havel charts its ethical resistance, **Milan Kundera** brings in its emotional weight. Through his concept of ***litost*** — a particularly Czech blend of sorrow, self-mockery, and introspection — he gives voice to the affective dimension of Bohemian existentialism. In *The Unbearable Lightness of Being*, Kundera offers what might be called an *"existential physics"*: a world where Nietzsche's *eternal return* is replaced by the unbearable singularity of lived experience. Here, history is both cosmically trivial and subjectively crushing. His characters do not overcome this paradox; they learn to inhabit it — what Kundera calls *"the wisdom of uncertainty,"* a disposition that echoes the Czech historical experience of enduring, adapting, and continuing under the shadow of unstable political realities.

## An End in Song and Irony

One final note on CK3 itself: during a developer livestream, a game director revealed that the tradition *"Life is Just a Joke"* has a hidden effect — not listed in the tooltip — where characters of that culture lose all accumulated stress just before execution. If a character of this culture is executed, they lose all accumulated stress just before death — a mechanic with no gameplay consequence, yet one that perfectly encapsulates the Bohemian worldview: **death not as terror, but as the ultimate punchline.**

Paradox, in this regard, truly did its homework — this tradition was not assigned arbitrarily but reflects something essential about the Czech spirit. As I conclude this exploration, I came across another game I had somehow overlooked — *Kingdom Come: Deliverance*, a narrative-rich RPG set in the Kingdom of Bohemia within the Holy Roman Empire.
