# Iberia or Iberia? — On Doubled Names and Divided Pasts

*An essay on doubled toponyms, geographical coincidence, and the strange power of names to collapse distance — inspired by a moment of confusion in Crusader Kings III.*

---

## A Map with a Stutter

I remember the exact moment the question lodged itself in my mind — one of those small, nagging confusions that begins as a footnote and slowly annexes the entire page. It was during one of my earlier CK3 campaigns, sometime around the release of the *"Fate of Iberia"* flavor pack, when Paradox Interactive introduced the *Struggle* mechanic to model the centuries-long interplay of Christian, Muslim, and Jewish cultures on the **Iberian Peninsula**. The pack was gorgeous — new decisions, cultural tensions, a dynamic system that made the **Reconquista** feel less like a scripted march of borders and more like the messy, generational tug-of-war it actually was. I was hooked immediately, as I often am when Paradox takes a region I thought I understood and reveals how little I actually knew.

But the seed of this essay was not planted by the Reconquista itself. It was planted by what happened when, mid-campaign as the **Kingdom of León**, I zoomed out — far out, past the Mediterranean, past Anatolia and the Black Sea — and noticed, nestled in the mountains of the **Caucasus**, another kingdom bearing an eerily familiar name: **Iberia**. Not a duchy or a county tucked into some corner of the map that might share a name by coincidence, but a full, distinct, historically attested kingdom — the same word, the same four syllables, applied to a land separated from Spain by over four thousand kilometers of sea, steppe, and empire.

I must have stared at the map for a good thirty seconds, toggling between the two regions, convinced I was misreading something. I was not. The western edge of Europe and the eastern edge of Europe both claimed the name *Iberia*, and neither, as far as I could tell, had borrowed it from the other. It was as if the ancient world had stuttered, uttering the same syllable twice and accidentally christening two entirely unrelated places with the same word — a toponymic echo across the breadth of the known world.

> ***How did two lands, separated by the entirety of the Mediterranean world, come to share the same name — and what, if anything, does that coincidence reveal about how the ancient world understood geography itself?***

---

## The Western Iberia: Where Continents Collide

Let us begin with the Iberia most readers will recognize — the great southwestern peninsula of Europe, that rugged, sun-scorched quadrilateral dangling from the Pyrenees into the Atlantic, encompassing modern **Spain**, **Portugal**, **Andorra**, **Gibraltar**, and a sliver of southern **France**. Geographically, the peninsula is a study in contrasts so stark they border on the theatrical: the arid, wind-blasted **Meseta Central**, a vast interior plateau that dominates the heart of the land like an elevated stage, gives way in the north to the rain-drenched green valleys of **Galicia** and the **Basque Country**, where Atlantic mists coil through mountain passes; in the south, the **Sierra Nevada** and the fertile lowlands of **Andalusia** stretch toward Africa across the narrow **Strait of Gibraltar**, a mere fourteen kilometers of turbulent water that has served, across millennia, as both barrier and bridge between continents. It is this geographic liminality — Europe's closest point to Africa, the Mediterranean's western gate, the Atlantic's southern shore — that made the peninsula not merely a destination but a crossroads, a place where civilizations did not simply arrive but *collided*, layered atop one another like geological strata.

The name itself reaches back to the ancient **Phoenicians** and **Greeks** who first sailed these coasts. The Greeks called it *Ibēría* (Ἰβηρία), a term likely derived from the **Ebro River** (*Ibēr* in Greek), the great watercourse that cuts across the peninsula's northeast — though some scholars have proposed a pre-Indo-European root, *ibar* or *iber*, meaning "river" in the language of the peninsula's indigenous inhabitants, suggesting that *Iberia* may simply have meant "the land of the river," a name so prosaic in origin that it borders on the poetic. The Romans, characteristically, preferred their own terminology — *Hispania* — but *Iberia* persisted in Greek geographical tradition, carried forward by writers like **Strabo**, **Herodotus**, and **Appian**, whose texts ensured the name would outlive the civilizations that coined it.

What makes western Iberia extraordinary, and what drew Paradox to dedicate an entire flavor pack to it, is the sheer *density* of civilizational layering compressed into a single peninsula. Consider the succession: **Phoenician** trading posts gave way to **Carthaginian** dominion under the **Barcids** (yes, *that* Hannibal's family), which fell to **Roman** conquest in the fires of the **Punic Wars**; Rome's centuries of rule produced emperors (**Trajan**, **Hadrian**, **Theodosius**), roads, aqueducts, and the Latin tongue that would eventually fracture into Castilian, Catalan, Portuguese, and Galician; the Western Empire's collapse opened the gates to the **Visigoths**, who established a Christian kingdom that lasted three centuries before the **Umayyad Caliphate**, in one of history's most dramatic pivots, swept across the Strait of Gibraltar in **711 CE** and dismantled Visigothic rule in barely seven years, establishing **Al-Andalus** — a civilization that would produce **Córdoba**'s Great Mosque, the poetry of **Ibn Hazm**, the philosophy of **Averroes**, and a model of interfaith coexistence (*convivencia*) that, however imperfect and romanticized, remains one of medieval Europe's most extraordinary experiments.

And then, of course, came the long unwinding — the **Reconquista**, that seven-century-long process (roughly **722–1492**) by which the Christian kingdoms of the north gradually, fitfully, and often violently reclaimed the peninsula from Muslim rule. It is this process that CK3's *Fate of Iberia* models, and rightly so, because the Reconquista was not a single war but a *civilizational rhythm* — decades of coexistence punctuated by bursts of holy war, truces bartered and broken, alliances that crossed religious lines as often as they reinforced them. The fall of **Granada** in **1492**, the same year Columbus sailed west, closed the chapter on Muslim Iberia and opened the age of global empire — a transition so dramatically timed that it feels scripted, though history, as we know, rarely bothers with narrative elegance.

## The Eastern Iberia: Kingdom in the Mountains

Now we turn eastward — far eastward, past the wine-dark Mediterranean, past the Aegean and the Bosporus, past the Anatolian plateau and the Armenian highlands, to the rugged folds of the **Caucasus Mountains**, where the *other* Iberia sits, ancient and proud and almost entirely unknown to the average Western reader. This is **Caucasian Iberia**, known in Georgian as **Kartli** (ქართლი), the heartland of what would eventually become the nation of **Georgia** — a kingdom whose history is as deep and turbulent as its western namesake's, yet which exists, for most Europeans, in a kind of cartographic blind spot, overshadowed by the empires that perpetually sought to swallow it.

The geography here is as dramatic as anything on the peninsula, though the drama is vertical rather than horizontal. The **Greater Caucasus** range — Europe's highest, surpassing even the Alps — forms a wall of ice and granite along the northern frontier, its peaks (**Elbrus**, **Kazbek**) piercing five thousand meters into thin air. To the south, the **Lesser Caucasus** ripples toward the Armenian and Anatolian plateaus, while between these ranges lies a corridor of river valleys, fertile lowlands, and strategic passes that made Kartli both coveted and defensible — a natural fortress not unlike Bohemia's mountain basin, though far more extreme in its verticality. The **Kura River**, Kartli's lifeline, winds through this corridor much as the Vltava threads through Bohemia, nourishing the capital **Mtskheta** (later **Tbilisi**) and connecting the highland interior to the Black Sea coast.

The name *Iberia* for this Caucasian kingdom comes not from the Georgians themselves — they called their land *Sakartvelo* and their heartland *Kartli* — but from the **Greeks** and **Romans** who encountered it. Classical authors, beginning with **Strabo** and **Pliny the Elder**, referred to the region as *Ibēría* (Ἰβηρία), using the exact same word they applied to the peninsula in the west. This was not confusion; Greek and Roman geographers were perfectly aware that two Iberias existed. Strabo, in his *Geographica*, explicitly distinguishes between them, calling one "Western Iberia" and the other "Eastern Iberia," with the matter-of-factness of a man who has accepted a toponymic inconvenience and moved on.

The history of Caucasian Iberia, compressed brutally, reads as a millennium-long negotiation between two gravitational fields: **Rome** (later **Byzantium**) pulling from the west, and **Persia** (the **Achaemenids**, then **Parthians**, then **Sassanids**) pulling from the east, with the Iberians caught perpetually in between, tilting one way or the other as the balance of power shifted. In the **4th century CE**, King **Mirian III** converted to Christianity — tradition credits **Saint Nino**, a Cappadocian woman, with the conversion — making Georgia one of the earliest Christian kingdoms in the world, predating even the formal Christianization of the Roman Empire under Theodosius. This early adoption of Christianity became, much as it did for the Armenians next door, the bedrock of national identity — a spiritual anchor that held even as political sovereignty was repeatedly lost to Persian fire-worshippers, Arab conquerors, Mongol horsemen, and Turkish sultans.

By the time CK3's map begins in **867 AD**, Caucasian Iberia had fragmented into competing principalities — the **Bagrationi** dynasty was slowly consolidating power, a process that would culminate in the unification of Georgia under **Bagrat III** around **1008** and the eventual golden age under **Queen Tamar** (r. 1184–1213), whose reign produced a cultural flowering so extraordinary — **Shota Rustaveli**'s epic poem *The Knight in the Panther's Skin*, soaring cathedral architecture, a sophisticated legal code — that Georgians regard it with the same reverence Czechs reserve for Charles IV's Prague or Hungarians for Stephen I's kingdom. It is one of history's quieter tragedies that this golden age was shattered by the Mongol invasions of the 1230s, and that Georgia's subsequent centuries were spent in a long, grinding diminishment under Timurid, Ottoman, and Persian domination — a trajectory heartbreakingly parallel to Hungary's post-Mohács partition.

## Why the Same Name?

So we arrive at the question that started this essay, the question I sat staring at on the CK3 map: *why?* Why did the ancient Greeks — careful geographers, compulsive categorizers, people who literally invented the word *geography* — apply the identical name to two regions separated by the entire breadth of their known world?

The honest answer is: **we don't know for certain**, and the uncertainty itself is part of what makes the question fascinating. Several theories have been proposed, none fully satisfying, each illuminating a different facet of how the ancient world processed and transmitted geographical knowledge:

**The etymological coincidence theory** holds that the two names derive from entirely separate linguistic roots that happen to sound alike — a false cognate on a continental scale. Western *Iberia*, as noted, likely derives from the Ebro (*Ibēr*) or from a pre-Indo-European word for "river." Eastern *Iberia* may derive from a Georgian or proto-Kartvelian root, or from an Iranian term — some scholars have linked it to the Middle Persian *Wiruzān* or *Vir(k)*, the Persian name for the Georgians, which Greek ears may have rendered as *Ibēr-*. Under this theory, the doubling is pure accident — two unrelated words, processed through the phonological filter of Greek, producing an identical output. Language, in this reading, played a prank on geography.

**The migration theory**, more speculative, suggests a genuine historical link — that an ancient people or tribal grouping called the *Iberi* once inhabited a broader range and, through migration or displacement, left traces of their name in both locations. This theory was popular in antiquity itself; Appian mentions it, and some Georgian chronicles embraced the connection, weaving it into national mythology. Modern scholarship treats this with skepticism — there is no linguistic, genetic, or archaeological evidence linking the pre-Roman inhabitants of Spain to the Kartvelians of the Caucasus — but the theory's persistence reveals something important about how societies *want* shared names to mean shared origins, how the human mind resists pure coincidence.

**The Greek cartographic convention theory** proposes that Greek geographers, encountering a mountainous, peripheral land at the eastern edge of their world that reminded them of the mountainous, peripheral land at the western edge, simply reused the name — much as European colonizers would later scatter "New" before familiar place names across the Americas. This is less a theory of etymology than of *cognitive geography*: the idea that the ancient Greek mental map was structured symmetrically, with the known world radiating outward from the Mediterranean center, and that lands at equivalent distances from that center — both mountainous, both inhabited by fierce, non-Greek peoples, both sitting at the edge of the *oikoumene* — might naturally attract the same label.

> ***Perhaps the truest answer is that the doubling of "Iberia" reveals not a fact about these two lands, but a fact about the minds that named them — that ancient geography was as much an act of pattern-making as of measurement, and that when the Greeks looked east and west toward the edges of their world, they saw, or wanted to see, a kind of symmetry.***

## The Deeper Pattern: Names That Echo

What began as a CK3 curiosity has, as these things tend to do with me, spiraled into a broader fascination — because the Iberian doubling, it turns out, is not unique. The ancient and medieval world is littered with toponymic echoes, doubled names that collapse vast distances into a single word, and once you start noticing them, you cannot stop:

**Galicia** — the name belongs simultaneously to the rainy, Celtic-tinged northwest corner of Spain (home of Santiago de Compostela and the end of the Camino) and to a region straddling modern **Poland** and **Ukraine** (the Galicia of the Habsburg *Kronland*, of Lviv and Kraków, of Hasidic Judaism and Polish nationalism). The two Galicias share no direct etymological link — the Spanish derives from the Roman *Gallaecia*, named for the Celtic **Gallaeci** tribe, while the eastern derives from the medieval Rus' principality of **Halych** — yet the coincidence has generated centuries of folk etymology and, occasionally, genuine confusion in historical texts.

**Albania** — today the name of the small Balkan nation on the Adriatic, but in antiquity, *Albania* (or *Caucasian Albania*) was also the name of a kingdom in the eastern Caucasus, roughly corresponding to modern **Azerbaijan**. Strabo, once again, writes about both. The Balkan Albania derives from the **Albanoi**, an Illyrian tribe mentioned by Ptolemy; the Caucasian Albania derives from a different root entirely. Two Albanias, two millennia, two mountain peoples, one word.

**Asia** — originally just the name of a small Roman province in western **Anatolia** (*Asia Minor*), derived perhaps from the Hittite *Assuwa*. The Greeks gradually extended it to mean the entire continent east of the Aegean — an act of toponymic imperialism so successful that we now use a word that once meant "the area around Ephesus" to describe a landmass stretching from Istanbul to Tokyo.

Each of these doublings tells a similar story: names are not neutral labels pinned to fixed locations. They are *living artifacts* of the cultures that coined them, carrying within their syllables the cognitive maps, the biases, the symmetries and blind spots of the people who first uttered them. When the Greeks called both Spain and Georgia "Iberia," they were not making a geographical error — they were revealing the structure of their own world-picture, one in which the Mediterranean was the center and everything else was periphery, and peripheries, by definition, rhyme.

## On the Power of Names

I want to close with something more personal, because this essay has, perhaps more than my previous ones, drifted from CK3 into territory that feels less like history and more like philosophy — but then, that drift is precisely what I find most rewarding about following these threads.

What strikes me most about the two Iberias is not the etymological puzzle — clever people have debated that for centuries and will continue to — but the *existential weight* that a shared name carries. When you learn that two places share a name, something shifts in your mental map. You begin to look for connections — cultural, geographical, spiritual — even when none exist. The mind, confronted with coincidence, manufactures meaning. And this impulse, I think, is not a flaw in human cognition but its defining feature: we are, before anything else, pattern-seekers, storytellers, creatures who cannot look at two dots without drawing a line between them.

The two Iberias have, as far as we can tell, no shared history, no common ancestry, no secret link waiting to be uncovered by some future archaeologist. They are, in all likelihood, a coincidence — two syllables, shaped by two unrelated languages, processed through the same Greek phonological system, and preserved by the same literary tradition. And yet the coincidence *means* something, not because it reveals a hidden truth about these lands, but because it reveals a truth about us: that we name the world not as it is, but as we perceive it, and that our names, once uttered, take on lives of their own — migrating, doubling, echoing across continents and centuries, long after the mouths that first spoke them have turned to dust.

In CK3, I can zoom out and see both Iberias on the same map, separated by a scroll wheel's worth of distance. In the real world, a person standing in **Córdoba**'s Mezquita and a person standing in **Tbilisi**'s sulfur baths occupy the same named space — *Iberia* — without sharing a single thread of common history. The word connects them and separates them simultaneously, a bridge built over an abyss, leading nowhere and everywhere at once.

> ***Names do not describe the world. They *propose* a world — and the two Iberias, sitting at opposite ends of Europe like bookends on a shelf of civilizations, remind us that every map is, at heart, a work of imagination.***
